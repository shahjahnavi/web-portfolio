Research Assistant




During my tenure as a Research Assistant at Rutgers University, working closely with Professor Joel Freundlich, I have been immersed in the captivating domain of HIV/AIDS research. My primary responsibility entails the manipulation and analysis of a vast dataset, comprising approximately 200,000 data points. My objective is to harness data-driven methodologies to construct predictive models for molecular inhibition.

Before embarking on the pivotal stages of regulatory data cleansing and exploratory data analysis (EDA), I grappled with the formidable challenge of data preprocessing, particularly in the realm of molecule standardization. The dataset presented a complex landscape rife with duplicate compounds, frequently arising from isomeric variations. To rectify this, I leveraged the robust capabilities of the RDKit library, thereby ensuring the normalization and standardization of these chemical entities.

However, the standardization process introduced computational bottlenecks, with runtimes exceeding 200 minutes for each iteration. Employing advanced optimization techniques, including mapping and block logging, managed to alleviate the issue to some extent, but it remained far from optimal. My quest for enhanced computational efficiency led me to the discovery of "pandarallel," a Python library that parallelizes data processing tasks, significantly reducing runtime overhead.

Delineating duplicate compounds posed yet another intricate challenge, demanding a nuanced approach to data deduplication. I adopted a criterion that prioritized the retention of compounds with higher inhibition values, ensuring the dataset's quality and relevance for subsequent analyses.

Subsequently, I embarked on the critical task of feature engineering, extracting essential molecular descriptors from the dataset. Given that the initial dataset only provided chemical formulae for each compound, this step was pivotal in preparing the data for advanced data science techniques.

Looking ahead, the research project is poised to advance into the realm of predictive modeling, employing regression techniques to forecast inhibition values for the diverse array of drugs encapsulated in the dataset. This phase of the project holds immense promise in yielding actionable insights that could significantly impact HIV/AIDS research, potentially unveiling novel avenues for therapeutic interventions and preventive measures.

My tenure as a Research Assistant has endowed me with a rich skill set encompassing data preprocessing, computational optimization, and the application of sophisticated data science methodologies. This experience has deepened my passion for research and fortified my commitment to driving transformative change in the healthcare and scientific research landscape.

Time Series Analysis of the S&P500 Index and Forecasting of Pfizer Stock Market Trends




The project begins by loading necessary libraries and then importing the Pfizer stock data from a CSV file. The data is cleaned and transformed, including converting the date column to the proper date format and adding new columns for month and year. Initial data exploration and visualization are performed, including plotting the closing price trends over time and by month.

The project focuses on time series analysis and forecasting using the ARIMA (AutoRegressive Integrated Moving Average) model. The data's stationarity is assessed through ACF (AutoCorrelation Function), PACF (Partial AutoCorrelation Function), and the Augmented Dickey-Fuller Test. The best-fitting ARIMA model is selected using the auto.arima function. The model's residuals are examined for autocorrelation and white noise properties. Finally, a five-year stock price forecast is generated with a 95% confidence interval.

The project aims to provide valuable insights into Pfizer's stock price trends and make future price predictions, aiding investors and financial analysts in their decision-making processes.

Libraries Used -
dplyr, tidyverse
